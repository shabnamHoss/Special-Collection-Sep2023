LEAF FROM HERBARIUS ZU TEUTSCH

Augsburg. 1499

From ancient times until the 20th century, the term materia medica was used to describe the substances and therapeutic properties of natural products used for medicinal purposes. Dioscorides, a 1st century Greek physician, wrote the first extensive pharmacopeia, a work which remained in use for 15 centuries and is the ancestor of our leaf. This single leaf, printed by Hans Schonsperger, is a German version of the Latin Hortus sanitatis published in Mainz. The hand-colored woodcut on this leaf, though printed in reverse, can also be seen in the Wellcome Collection’s Herbarius. Notable features in this edition include double columns for text and smaller illustrations, changes made to reduce the amount of paper needed to print the book. It’s difficult to identify this plant even with the help of the caption on the verso of the leaf.

THE ECLECTIC PHYSIOLOGY OR GUIDE TO HEALTH

New York. 1888

Many books on medical topics printed in the 19th century were published as schoolbooks. By 1850, instruction in physiology, hygiene, and physical training were standard part of education curricula. All three of these illustrated texts include basic information about anatomy and major bodily functions but the books produced in the 1880s emphasize the negative role alcohol, tobacco, and narcotics play in human health. This shift is due in part to the Woman’s Christian Temperance Union support for state legislation requiring temperance instruction in public schools.

GOOD HEALTH MORE VALUABLE THAN GREAT RICHES

D. Mortimore

Memphis. 1867

In 19th century Tennessee, as was the case elsewhere in the country, doctors offering cures for every imaginable disease peddled their products and services. Mortimore was one of these. Tennessee didn’t require licensing of physicians until 1889 so potential patients had no way of verifying the credibility of the practitioner or the cure except through testimonials like the ones filling the pages of this pamphlet.

THE BOTANIC PHYSICIAN, OR FAMILY MEDICAL ADVISER

J. E. Carter

Madisonville, Tennessee. 1837

CHOLERA AS IT APPEARED IN NASHVILLE

W. K. Bowling

Nashville. 1866

Unlike Mortimore, William K. Bowling’s credentials are well-known. He taught at the Medical Department of the University of Nashville and founded the Nashville Journal of Medicine and Surgery in 1851. This pamphlet is one of a number he wrote about the periodic cholera epidemics and their effect on public health.

THE HUMAN BODY

Richard Walker and Rachel

London. 2018

Sixteenth century anatomy books were some of the first books to utilize movable elements, specifically flaps, which allowed the reader to “dissect” the body by lifting layers of skin and muscle to reveal the organs underneath. At first, these movable books were produced for a select circle of anatomists and physicians but by the 19th century, flap books were common teaching tools used by aspiring medical practitioners as well as laymen. Today, pop-ups add a new, three-dimensional element to popular anatomy books designed for children and adults. This Victorian inspired example utilizes unique, interlocking folded layers combined with simple flaps to create a complex, deep dive down into the workings of human physiology.

PHYSIOLOGY AND CALISTHENICS

Catharine E. Beecher

New York. 1858

MEDICAL ADVISER

Rezin Thompson

Germantown, Tennessee. 1860

Early 19th century publishing in Tennessee included numerous examples of practical medical works. Although he doesn’t reference Samuel Thomson’s botanicism specifically, Carter was clearly a proponent of his plant-based, populist approach to medicine. If it is true, as Carter states in his introduction, that “the same earth which yields our food, produces our medicine,” then his book’s materia medica section and dispensatory with more than 200 recipes should prepare anyone with plain common sense to follow instructions and create their own remedies. Thompson’s Medical Adviser, like Carter’s work, addresses a broad audience but he drops the botanic emphasis and provides a much more substantive discussion of human diseases.

HORSE-BACK RIDING FROM A MEDICAL POINT OF VIEW

New York. 1878

The Warden Memorial Equine collection contains a plethora of work on horse health and well-being, but this small volume addresses the medical status of the rider, not the horse. Exercise is an obvious benefit of riding. Durant goes further, explaining its salutary effect on circulation of the blood, respiration, digestion, and perspiration. His list of diseases for which riding is therapeutic includes anemia, syphilis, gout, diabetes, and fever.
